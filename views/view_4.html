Evolution of Average Labour Productivity after World War II

Description: Below shows a line plot illustrating the average hourly labour productivity (verticle axis, in [$/hour]) over time (horizontal axis, in [year]), recorded after World War II. The plot involves a single line representing the change of average hourly labour productivity over years, for a particle selected country. The line color reflects the country's continent consistent with previous slides.

NOTE: Try (re)selecting a country from the dropdown menu below to see how its labour productivity changes over years.

According to the line plot above, one can infer that:

  • Almost all countries shown in the data set experience growth of average labour productivity since the end of World War II; however, some countries grow quickly (mostly in Europe, North America and Oceania), while others (mostly in South America, Africa and Asia) grow much slower or go ups and downs.
  • The time of notable increase of labour productivity growth rate (especially after 1980) of most countries is aligned with the improvements and universalization of electronic computers, as well as the enhancement of international collaboration. Therefore, technology advancement and economic globalization can be two important drivers of labour productivity growth, which eventually lead to shorter average working hours.
  • In the US, for example, farm production per labour hour increased nearly 16-fold from 1948 to 2011. This increased productivity enables the US to feed a rapidly growing population since the end of World War II, even while the fraction of people working in agriculture constantly decreases. Meanwhile, the improvement of agricultural efficiency associated with increased productivity enables shorter working hours to achieve certain agricultural output.
  • Finally, different countries in different regions benefit from technology progress and economic globalization with different extent, as reflected by their significantly different productivity growth rates over years. In general, North American, European, Oceanian and some East Asian countries benefit from the two factors the most. Hence, the difference in productivity and economic prosperity among countries may not be fully attributed to their work ethic and diligence, but can be the differences in opportunities and politics.
