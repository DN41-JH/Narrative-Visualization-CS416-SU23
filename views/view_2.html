Average Annual Working Hours vs. GDP per Capita (2019)

Description: Below shows a scatter plot illustrating the average annual working hours (horizontal axis, in [hour]) versus the average GDP per capita (verticle axis, in [$/year]), in the year of 2019. Each bubble in the plot represents a distinct country, with the bubble size scaled by its total population, and colored according to continent as shown in the legend (top right corner).

NOTE: Try hovering over any bubble, and you will see the annual hours worked along with its GDP per capita in 2019:

According to the scatter plot above, one may infer that:

  • In general, workers in more developed and richer countries (mostly in Europe, North America and part of Asia) tend to work fewer hours than workers in poorer and less developed countries.
  • The reason could be that workers in more developed countries yield (much) higher productivity in an unit of work time in their economy system, as elaborated in the next slide. As a result, workers in more developed countries may obtain higher incomes with even shorter work time, compared with their counterparts in less developed countries, therefore having more space and opportunities for self-development or relaxation before/after work.
  • The distribution of work-life-balance as well as economic income is highly uneven across the world: workers in less developed countries work longer, but receive less income compared with their counterparts in more developed countries. This set of data makes us rethink of our notions of economic globalization and wealth distribution over the last century, as well as the nature and cause of significant economic/work inequality among countries and regions.
