Average Annual Working Hours vs. Hourly Labour Productivity

Description: Below shows a scatter plot illustrating the average annual working hours (horizontal axis, in [hour]) versus the average hourly labour productivity (verticle axis, in [$/hour]), in the year of 2019. Each bubble in the plot represents a distinct country, with the bubble size scaled by its total population, and colored according to continent as shown in the legend (top right corner).

Elaboration: In this case, the hourly labour productivity is defined as the economic return (i.e. money earned) for one hour of work. At the most concrete level, for example, labour productivity can represent the value of cars that an average automobile factory workers assemble in an hour, or the value of food that an average restaurant cook make in one hour, etc.

NOTE: Try hovering over any bubble, and you will see the annual hours worked along with its average hourly labour productivity in 2019:

According to the scatter plot above, one can observe that:

  • Higher average productivity usually corresponds to higher GDP per capita as well, as the countries of higher labour productivity align with higher average annual GDP countries shown in previous slides (again, mostly in Europe, North America, Oceania and part of Asia).
  • Higher labour productivity represents higher economic development level, but is (in general) associated with fewer working hours, which is especially the case for countries in Europe (blue), North America (green) and Oceania (brown). The exceptions are mostly in East Asia (Hong Kong and Singapore, etc.), whose average working hour is notably higher than their counterpart contries in Europe and North America with similar high productivity.
  • An explanation of such high productivity — low working hours correlation could be that, higher productivity per hour provides workers with higher incomes and therefore allows them to work shorter time to reach the desired and comfortable standard of living. The exception of some East Asian countries (i.e. Hong Kong and Singapore) is more likely to be attributed to cultural aspect.
  • The key driver of rising incomes and decreasing working hours is productivity growth; so it is important to investigate the evolution of labour productivity over time (as in the next slide), before a comprehensive understanding on the notably income and productivity differences among countries can be reached.
